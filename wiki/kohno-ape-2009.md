# Kohno et al. APE 2009

**Summary**: First ¹⁷¹Yb optical lattice clock at NMIJ (Japan), measuring the clock frequency to 28 Hz uncertainty and finding 759.353(3) nm magic wavelength — providing independent confirmation of the NIST Lemke 2009 result within 1.1 Hz.

**Sources**: T Kohno et al. APE 2009.pdf

**Last updated**: 2026-04-26

---

## Reference

T. Kohno, M. Yasuda, K. Hosaka, H. Inaba, Y. Nakajima, and F.-L. Hong, "One-Dimensional Optical Lattice Clock with a Fermionic ¹⁷¹Yb Isotope," *Applied Physics Express* **2**, 072501 (2009).

## Context

NMIJ (National Metrology Institute of Japan) built an independent ¹⁷¹Yb optical lattice clock to complement the NIST work. The primary goal was to demonstrate a second independent measurement of the Yb clock frequency and magic wavelength, validating or flagging systematic errors in either system. The paper is notable as the first non-NIST Yb lattice clock result.

## Key Results

### Absolute Frequency
$$\nu_{^{171}\text{Yb}} = 518,295,836,590,864(28) \text{ Hz}$$

The 28 Hz uncertainty is ~40× larger than Lemke 2009, primarily because the optical frequency comb was referenced to a H-maser rather than a primary Cs fountain standard. Agreement with Lemke 2009 (518,295,836,590,865.2(0.7) Hz) is within 1.2 Hz — excellent given the NMIJ uncertainty.

### Magic Wavelength
$$\lambda_\text{magic}(^{171}\text{Yb}) = 759.353(3) \text{ nm}$$

Consistent with the Lemke 2009 value of ~759.354 nm (more precisely, $\nu_\text{magic} = 394,798,329(10)$ MHz).

## Experimental Setup

- **Laser source at 578 nm**: Sum-frequency generation (SFG) — 1030 nm Yb:YAG + 1319 nm Nd:YAG → 578 nm via PPLN (periodically-poled lithium niobate) waveguide. This all-solid-state approach contrasts with the NIST dye laser and became the basis for many subsequent groups.
- **Reference**: H-maser + GPS comparison for frequency measurement
- **Cooling**: Two-stage MOT similar to NIST (399 nm + 556 nm)
- **Interrogation time**: ~80 ms Rabi pulses

## SFG Laser Source

The SFG approach is notable: 578 nm is not available from commercial diode or solid-state lasers, but can be generated by sum-frequency mixing two common solid-state laser wavelengths. The PPLN waveguide allows efficient SFG at low pump power. This approach is more robust and alignment-insensitive than dye laser systems and has been adopted by subsequent Yb clock groups (ECNU, etc.).

## Consistency Check with Lemke 2009

The NMIJ value agrees with NIST to within their combined uncertainties, providing important independent verification that:
1. No common systematic error exists at the few-Hz level
2. The electron shelving detection protocol gives consistent results across setups
3. The magic wavelength is not apparatus-dependent
